How this pregnancy weight gain calculator works

The tool calculates pre-pregnancy BMI from height and weight, maps that BMI to the CDC/IOM category, and displays the corresponding total pregnancy gain range. For singleton pregnancies it also shows a broad planning trajectory using first-trimester and second/third-trimester guideline rates.

pre-pregnancy BMI = weight(lb) ÷ height(in)² × 703weight gained = current weight − pre-pregnancy weight

The singleton trajectory uses approximately 1.1–4.4 pounds total during weeks 1–13, followed by category-specific weekly ranges. It is not a diagnosis, a required weekly target, or a substitute for the growth chart used by a prenatal clinician.

Understanding your results

Weight is only one part of prenatal assessment. Fluid shifts, fetal and placental growth, blood volume, nausea, illness, edema, measurement timing, and multiple pregnancy all affect the scale. Being outside a calculated band does not establish that anything is wrong.

Singleton and twin total ranges

For one baby, CDC totals are 28–40 lb for underweight, 25–35 lb for normal BMI, 15–25 lb for overweight, and 11–20 lb for obesity. For twins, CDC lists 50–62, 37–54, 31–50, and 25–42 lb respectively; the underweight twin figure comes from a separate cited study.

Frequently asked questions about pregnancy weight gain

How much weight should I gain during pregnancy based on BMI?

The recommended total depends on pre-pregnancy BMI and the number of babies. CDC ranges for one baby run from 11–20 pounds for BMI 30 or higher to 28–40 pounds for BMI below 18.5. Your clinician may set a different goal based on your health and pregnancy.

How much weight is typical in the first trimester?

ACOG says some people gain 1–5 pounds during the first 12 weeks, while others gain none. Nausea, vomiting, starting BMI, and individual health can change the pattern. Persistent vomiting, dehydration, rapid changes, swelling, or concern should be discussed promptly with prenatal care.

Are weekly recommendations reliable for twins?

Twin pregnancies have higher total recommended ranges, but a simple weekly interpolation cannot reflect fetal growth, fluid, complications, or individualized obstetric care. This calculator therefore avoids presenting a twin weekly target and reports the CDC total range only.

Important medical disclaimer

This tool does not provide medical advice or assess maternal or fetal health. Use it only for education. Discuss weight goals with your obstetric clinician. Seek urgent medical advice for sudden swelling or weight change, severe headache, vision changes, persistent vomiting, bleeding, severe pain, reduced fetal movement, or any emergency concern.
